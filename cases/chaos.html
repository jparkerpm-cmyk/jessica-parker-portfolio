Protecting Delivery from Organizational Chaos

Situation

The company was a smaller organization finding its footing inside a larger parent company. It worked with major pharmaceutical manufacturers, and when a manufacturer wanted something, leadership felt pressure to deliver it immediately. On a weekly to monthly basis, C-suite or upper management would arrive with a new initiative that needed to fit into the existing roadmap. The team was constantly being asked to absorb new work without anything being removed.

Diagnosis

The problem was not the incoming requests. Those were legitimate business needs. The problem was that no one had a framework for evaluating trade-offs. Every new initiative was treated as additive.

Without a visible, agreed-upon picture of what the team was already carrying, leadership could not make informed decisions. They were managing by urgency, not by strategy.

What I Did

For every incoming request, the framework ran the same way every time.

01 Assessed the request first. Did I have enough information to t-shirt size it, or did I need to gather that before anything else?
02 Brought every request to the team before making any commitment. No commitments without the team's input on feasibility and impact.
03 Evaluated the request against current work in progress. Named the conflict explicitly.
04 Presented leadership with clear trade-offs. Not a problem. A decision with options.
05 Negotiated scope and timing until we had an agreement the team could deliver on. Then held that agreement.

How a trade-off conversation sounded

"You want this medium-sized initiative. We are doing this large initiative. I can do one or the other. Or I can start the medium and pause the large. Or I can do an MVP of each if we agree on what MVP means. Here is what I think it means."

Results

 The team absorbed 1 to 2 epic-sized scope changes per month without missing a delivery commitment
 Every negotiated scope shipped on time. When original scope was reduced to fit a new initiative, that reduction was explicit, agreed upon, and the new scope delivered in full
 Team morale improved measurably and outpaced peer teams in the same organization not using the framework
 Leadership stopped managing by urgency. Trade-off conversations replaced scope-piling

The frustration with constant change did not disappear. What disappeared was the feeling of being ambushed. The team knew they would never be asked to take on what they could not handle. That was the shift.

This only worked because leadership was willing to engage with the trade-offs instead of overriding them. My part was making sure the trade-off was always on the table. Leaders make different decisions when the cost of change is in front of them.
