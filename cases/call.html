Case Study 06

After the Call, the Patient Went Blind

Direct-to-Patient Digital Pharmacy · Prescription Status Experience

Patient Flow Workflow Gap Diagnosis Direct-to-Patient Pharmacy Prescription Fulfillment B2B2C

Situation

A direct-to-patient digital pharmacy. The prescription ships, and then one of three things goes wrong. The patient gets the wrong medication, usually the wrong dose, easy to do when they have several similar meds at different strengths and order the one they did not mean to. Or it arrives damaged or out of temperature range and cannot be used. Or it never arrives at all.

In every case the patient is holding a problem. The wrong thing, an unusable thing, or nothing. What they need is a replacement and the confidence that it is actually coming.

Where the System Went Quiet

The fix itself required Ops and a phone call. A person had to process the refund or place the replacement order. That part was reasonable. The patient made the call, explained what happened, and Ops took it from there.

Then the patient went blind.

And remember who this patient is. Their medication did not come. They have not taken a dose they were supposed to have already taken. They are worried, and they are right to be. Once the call ended, the product showed them nothing. Did the refund process or is it still pending. Was the replacement actually placed. If it was, has it shipped, where is it, when does it land. They had no way to know. So they called again. And again. The first call was unavoidable. The follow-up calls, the ones asking whether the thing they were promised had happened yet, were the real volume, and they came from people watching the clock on a dose they did not have.

The Diagnosis

That call load was read as an ops cost. I read it as the product abandoning the patient at the worst possible moment. A patient with the wrong dose in their hands is not a support ticket. That is a person who cannot safely take their medication, who has already done the one thing the system asked of them, and who is now reduced to dialing a stranger to confirm a stranger did their job.

The fix still ran through Ops. The visibility into it now belonged to the patient.

I built the status experience to own the part the call could not. After the patient reported the problem, the product showed them the outcome moving. If a refund was issued, whether it had processed. If a replacement was placed, where it was in the process and its ETA. If the path was a refund plus a new order, whether that order had gone through.

Results

The company measured the drop in inbound calls, and it dropped without a headcount ask. The result that mattered was quieter. A patient who had received the wrong medication could see, on their own, that the right one was on its way and when it would arrive. They no longer had to chase it.

A patient waiting on a replacement is not waiting on a package. They are waiting on a dose they should already have taken, and every hour of silence is an hour of not knowing whether the fix is real. The product should carry that moment, not hand it back to the person least able to carry it. I built it so it did.
