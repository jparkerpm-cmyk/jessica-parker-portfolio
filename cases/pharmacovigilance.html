Situation

A specialty pharmacy services company sold medication management services to pharmaceutical manufacturers. One of those services was adverse event and product quality complaint reporting. When a patient called about a problem with their medication, a customer service rep needed to log it, route it to the right people, and get it to the manufacturer within 24 hours. That is a regulatory requirement. Missing it is not a paperwork problem. It is a compliance problem.

They were tracking everything in Excel. Five people touched every report before it got out the door. Five HIPAA incidents per month on average. No consistent reporting structure. The 24-hour SLA was a manual chase. PHI routed to the wrong recipients is not only a compliance failure. It is a patient safety risk.

What We Built

A single internal tool built within the company's existing CRM platform for customer service reps to log adverse events and product quality complaints in real time, on the call. One form per manufacturer, with dynamic fields that updated based on medication and medication type. Standardized inputs eliminated the variability that had been creating compliance exposure.

 AE/PQC review queue for the compliance team to catch missing data before submission
 Automated routing to the correct manufacturer third party, coded per client so the wrong report never went to the wrong place
 Audit-ready database with reporting built for internal review, manufacturer reporting, and regulatory inspection

The Ask That Opened a Gap

Mid-project a stakeholder came to me asking for an automated email. A specific edge case: manufacturers who had not subscribed to the AE/PQC service. She still needed a way to track those patient reports internally without routing them to the third party.

I asked why.

The email was a workaround she had invented because the gap had no name. Naming it changed what we built.

What she needed was not an email. She needed a workflow path that did not exist yet. A whole category of manufacturer clients had no home in the system we were building. When a manufacturer had not subscribed to the service, patient calls still came in. Those reports still had regulatory requirements attached. The product had no answer for them.

We added a separate workflow path for non-subscribing manufacturers. Their patient reports now had a compliant place to go. Nothing fell through.

On Scope

The original ask was to solve the compliance problem. Every patient call with an adverse event or product quality complaint had a regulatory requirement attached to it. That was true whether the manufacturer had subscribed to the AE/PQC service or not. The non-subscribing path was not a new problem. It was part of the same problem that had not been named yet.

The stakeholder did not walk in asking for a new workflow. She walked in asking for an email because that was the solution she could see from where she was sitting. The discovery work is what surfaced what she actually needed.

Discovery does not stop when the build starts. What comes in mid-project gets evaluated the same way anything else does: does this belong in what we are building now, does it belong in a future iteration, or is it something else entirely. The PRD captures what you know at the start. Discovery is how you find what you did not know to ask.

Results

Adoption was immediate.

 Processing time dropped 50%
 24-hour SLA went from a manual chase to a system guarantee
 HIPAA incidents dropped from 5 per month to zero
 Five people in the routing chain became two
 Audits stopped being a scramble
 Non-subscribing manufacturer reports had a compliant path for the first time
 No client issues after release
