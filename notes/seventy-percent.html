The Technology Was the Easy Part.

On why the 70% nobody scoped is where a healthcare AI rollout is won

BCG puts a number on healthcare AI rollouts. Ten percent of the effort is the algorithm. Twenty percent is the technology and data. The remaining 70% is people and process. Add the first two and the build is 30% of what makes a rollout work. The other 70% is whether the clinicians who have to use the tool use it.

Most systems already own the 30%. The model, the integration, the infrastructure. Bought. What is left is the 70%, and it is the best work in health tech.

Leadership treats adoption as the finish line. A clinician logs in, the dashboard turns green, the rollout gets called a win. The usage dashboard lies. It tells you people logged in. It does not tell you the tool cost them ten minutes they did not have, or that they are charting around it after the shift.

Clinicians are conditional. They keep a tool that reduces work and fits the shift. They push back on anything that adds steps, review, or risk. Poorly implemented AI drives burnout and turnover. Well-implemented AI improves retention. The deciding factor is not the model. It is whether the tool matched the work.

Login counts measure the 30%. Burnout measures the 70%.

So measure the shift, not the login. When a tool fights the clinician, burnout climbs. When it fades into the work, burnout drops. Time-to-action, manual follow-ups removed, documentation turnaround, after-hours charting. Measure it before the tool arrives, then measure it after. When the number moves the right way, you have proof the tool earned its place.

Closing the 70% is workflow design. Understand the clinician's day. Map where the tool adds load and remove the load before launch. Tie success to a lighter shift, not a login. This is product work, and it runs from before the build through after the launch.

If you bought the platform and the numbers are flat, good. The hard money is spent and the best part is still ahead. The 70% is findable, nameable, and buildable. Give a nurse a tool that disappears into the shift and you give her time back with patients. Start there.
