Field Notes

The platform shipped. The clinicians are using their phones instead.

June 2026

72% of US clinicians turn to personal AI tools when the workplace option does not meet their needs. That number comes from the Philips Future Health Index 2026. The same survey reports that 70% of clinicians say their AI training is inadequate, inconsistent, or unavailable.

The training number gets the headline. The personal-tool number is the one that matters.

75% of US health systems have deployed at least one AI solution, up from 59% a year earlier, and half are running three or more. The buying decision is done. The money is spent. And most of it stalls. MIT's NANDA Initiative found that only 5% of generative AI pilots reach rapid production impact. The failure point is almost never the model. It is the workflow the model got dropped into.

A clinician reaching for a personal AI tool on shift is not a policy violation waiting to be caught. She is a signal. She had a task, the sanctioned system did not serve it, and she found something that did. The personal tool filled a gap the organization created and cannot see.

Most leadership reads this behavior as a governance failure. The response follows a pattern. Lock down access. Issue a policy. Schedule a training. Add a reviewer to catch the workaround. Each of those treats the clinician as the problem. None of them touches the reason she reached for the tool in the first place.

The reason is structural. The workflow assumed something that was not true. It assumed the clinician had time she did not have. It assumed she had context the system never gave her. It assumed a handoff between steps that does not hold when the floor is busy. The AI did not fail. The workflow it was built into failed, and the AI inherited the failure.

I see this pattern from three angles at once. I was the nurse at the bedside, three patients behind, deciding in two seconds whether the system was worth opening. I was the developer who built the tool. I was the PM who decided whether it shipped. The model that looks clean in testing breaks under load. The alert that fires too often gets ignored. The workflow that assumes spare time gets abandoned for the thing on the phone that takes ten seconds.

Closing the gap starts with finding it. Map where clinicians reach for personal tools. Each of those points is a place the sanctioned workflow failed a specific task under real conditions. Name the structural cause for each one. Then rebuild that part of the workflow to match how the work actually happens on the floor, under real load.

RAND studied why AI projects fail and named five root causes. Only one is technical. The other four are organizational, strategic, and procedural. The organizations buying platforms right now are spending against the one technical cause and leaving the four that actually sink the project unaddressed. The gap stays open and the clinicians keep using their phones.

If your AI is live and adoption is flat, the answer is in the workflow. Find where it broke. Close it there.
